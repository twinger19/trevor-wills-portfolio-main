# Portfolio Audit: Positioning for Director-Level Creative Leadership

**Prepared for:** Trevor Wills
**Audience lens:** Creative hiring manager / Head of Brand / design-literate executive recruiter scanning for Creative Director, Group Creative Director, Brand Director, Director of Design, or Senior Design Director at a bigger brand.
**Scan time assumed:** 90–120 seconds
**Date:** April 2026

---

## Executive Summary

The site is well-built, coherent, and tasteful. It convincingly establishes a Senior Design Director who runs a complex seasonal creative process across major heritage brands. For a Director-tier hiring manager, that foundation is real — you are in the defensible zone for every role on your target list. The audit below is about closing the shortlist gap, not climbing a tier.

Three structural issues are keeping the site one notch below where it could read:

1. **The narrative is experiential rather than capability-driven.** The homepage leads with "15+ years" and your name in 72pt type. At Director level, every finalist has the years. What separates candidates is a crisp claim about *what you are specifically good at* and *what changes when you're in the room*.
2. **The case studies are process-heavy and outcome-light.** Five of six read like documentation of how work moves through your hands, not what changed because you ran it. Hiring managers at Director level accept that full P&L disclosure is rare, but they expect *some* numbers — sell-through, speed-to-market, team growth, door counts, launch outcomes, adoption rates.
3. **The visual system is tasteful-template, not proprietary.** Playfair Display + terracotta + rounded cards reads as "refined designer" rather than "creative leader with a point of view." For a creative hiring manager specifically, this is the layer they judge hardest. Your site should feel like a small brand, not a theme.

Everything else (information architecture, SEO, image quality, case-study breadth) is solid foundation. The audit below explains what to cut, what to amplify, and what to refine.

## 1. Value Proposition & Narrative

### What is working

Your homepage establishes credibility fast. The eyebrow ("Senior Design Director"), the brand wall (Levi's, Calvin Klein, Tommy Hilfiger, Columbia, Target, Walmart, Costco), and the immediate jump to six case studies all do work. A recruiter scrolling for three seconds will register "senior, multi-brand, retail-fluent." That is a real asset at Director tier.

### Where it falls short

The hero is where the positioning problem lives. Right now the hierarchy on the page is:

> **Senior Design Director**
> **I'm Trevor Wills**
> *15+ years leading creative for the world's most recognized heritage brands*

The largest piece of type on your site is your name. That is a creative's convention, not a leader's. A hiring manager who lands here learns "a person with experience." They do not learn *why you specifically*.

The subtitle that follows describes what you do ("I transform complex brand challenges into cohesive creative strategies…"). It is competent, but every Director-level portfolio says some version of this. A Creative Director search is almost always a *fit* search — the hiring manager is looking for a specific flavor of leader (maker-leader vs. strategist-leader vs. operator-leader) — and your site does not signal which one you are.

There is also an internal inconsistency worth fixing. Your structured data schema says "Senior Design Director." Your meta description and OG tags say "Creative Strategy Director." Your hero eyebrow says "Senior Design Director." Your About page says "Creative Strategy Director." Pick one, and be deliberate about it. "Senior Design Director" is accurate and defensible. "Creative Strategy Director" is the stronger positioning for roles that emphasize brand strategy over team management. Decide which roles you're hunting first, then align the title to them.

### Biggest single fix: replace the hero with a capability claim

Experience framing (current):

> I'm Trevor Wills
> 15+ years leading creative for the world's most recognized heritage brands

Director-tier framing (recommended pattern):

> **I direct brand creative that holds up in the work — and on the P&L.**
> Senior Design Director at Randa Apparel & Accessories. Creative lead for Levi's, Calvin Klein, Tommy Hilfiger, Columbia, Cole Haan, Tommy Bahama, and Dickies. Founded and scaled Damen + Hastings from pop-up to Kohl's.
> *Open to Creative Director, Group Creative Director, and Brand Director roles.*

Why this works for Director tier specifically:

- The first line stakes a claim about *what you are good at*: creative that is both craft-strong and commercially serious. That is the exact dual strength a Creative Director search is trying to test for.
- The second line is proof stack. It front-loads the brand roster and the entrepreneurial credential side-by-side, which is rare and differentiating at Director level.
- The third line handles positioning without dialing up to VP language. It names the roles you want.
- "Trevor Wills" belongs in the wordmark/nav, where a reader expects a name — not as the headline.

### Rework the three-stat block

Today you show `15+ / 7+ / 10+` — years, brands, designers mentored. Years is a commodity, brands is already in the logo wall below, and "designers mentored" is vague. Swap to metrics a Director-level hiring manager actually uses:

- **Brand scope** ("7 global heritage brands directed" or "$100M+ wholesale portfolio under creative direction" — ranges are fine)
- **Process outcome** ("35% faster speed-to-market via 3D adoption" — you already own this number, elevate it)
- **Team leadership** ("Direct reports: 4 senior designers across US and China" or "Promoted 4 designers to senior roles")

You don't need to reach for dollar figures you can't defend. Specificity wins over magnitude at Director tier. A line like "Directed 6+ bi-annual collections across 7 heritage brands at $XXXM wholesale volume" does more for you than "15+ years."

### Closing the homepage

The Charles Eames quote at the bottom of the homepage is pleasant but borrowed. It is the last impression a scanner takes away. Replace it with a short "What I believe about creative leadership" block — three to five lines in your own voice. Not a published essay, not a thesis piece, just the stance. Something like:

> **On how I work**
> Strategy before craft. Protect the designer's judgment. Build the system that lets the next team win without you. Brand is a promise the whole organization has to keep.

That kind of block is within reach for a Director candidate and signals a point of view without demanding thought-leadership scaffolding (speaking engagements, published essays) that you don't need at this tier.

## 2. Case Study Depth — Output vs. Outcome

This is where the largest uplift is available, and it is true at every altitude: the work has to tell the reader what changed. Of your six case studies, only one (**Pioneering Digital Product Creation**) reads like a Director-grade case study. Here is the honest read, case by case.

### Case-by-case grade

**Creative Direction for Heritage Brands — Output-framed.** The piece walks me through your seasonal process: trend → design → tech pack → sample → campaign. Every carousel is a stage of the production pipeline. The "Key Outcomes" section says you launched "6+ complete seasonal collections across 7+ heritage brands." That is a count of deliverables. Director-grade outcome language looks like: wholesale growth rate, sell-through at a named retail partner, retention of a licensor contract, a brand you repositioned, a category share movement. Even one defensible number changes the read.

**Seasonal Trend Development — Output-framed.** Similar issue. The case study documents activity (trend books, showroom experiences, client presentations). The absence of buyer response, order lift, or retailer adoption leaves the reader to infer value. Infer is the wrong verb for a case study.

**Pioneering Digital Product Creation — Outcome-framed. This is your model.** You lead with a stats showcase (60% sample reduction, 6 weeks faster development, 75% digital approvals) and then walk through the decisions that produced them. Every other case study on the site should be rebuilt against this template. The only thing missing is a human beat — "I hired a full-time 3D designer in our China office and we trained the broader team against the standards we set." That sentence is already almost on the page; surface it.

**Building a Vertical Brand: Damen + Hastings — Narrative-rich, outcome-vague.** This is your most strategically valuable case study — it is the one that proves entrepreneurial range and P&L proximity. It is also the one most in need of numbers. "From pop-up to Kohl's" is a compelling arc, but a Director-level hiring manager wants to see: How many doors at Kohl's? What sell-through at launch? First-year revenue range? How many SKUs at peak? Did it reorder? If any of that is defensible, put it on the page. If specific numbers are confidential, give ranges ("launched into 500+ doors"; "first-year wholesale in the low eight figures") and frame them as directional. The current version reads as a beautifully written essay with the commercial receipts held back.

**Strategic Brand Assessment for Walmart — Outcome implied, never stated.** The line "millions of dollars in potential new business" is the one place you quantify impact, and it is hedged to the point of invisibility. Either you landed the work (say what you landed, with door count and program name), or you didn't (say what the concrete next step is — LOI, test program, assortment lock). "Potential" language tells a recruiter that the deal did not close or that you're not allowed to say it did. Either way, trim the hedges.

**AI-Powered Trend Presentation — Currently a weaker entry than the others.** This case study is self-referential: you used Claude to build a trend site, and you're showing that site as evidence. It is charming, and at Director tier the bar is lower on "thought leadership" artifacts than at VP — but it currently reads as a hobby project alongside five enterprise-scale stories. Either strengthen the business framing (tie it explicitly to your AI Trailblazers role at RAA — what you rolled out at the company, who adopted it, what hours or dollars it saved, what governance you created) or demote it from the homepage grid to a "Notes" or "Experiments" section.

### A template to refit every case study against

Most Director-grade case studies share the same skeleton. Apply this to every story on the site:

1. **Context (1 sentence):** The business problem, in commercial or brand terms, not creative terms. Example: "RAA's accessories business was under pressure from Target and Walmart mandates requiring 3D sampling that our category had never delivered."
2. **Mandate (1 sentence):** What *you specifically* were asked to own. Title, scope, team, timeline.
3. **Decision (2–3 sentences):** The strategic choice *you* made — and the option you rejected. This is the moment that distinguishes a leader from an executor.
4. **Collaboration (1–2 sentences):** Who had to agree — VP of Merchandising? Brand licensor? Sourcing lead? Target's 3D product creation team? Named partners make your soft power visible.
5. **Team (1 sentence):** Who you led, how many, where, and who you elevated during the engagement.
6. **Outcome (bulleted, numeric):** At least one number — in dollars, percentage points, time, doors, sell-through, or adoption.
7. **What it changed at the organization after you (1 sentence):** Did a process stick? Did a team persist? Did a brand standard get adopted elsewhere? This is how a single project becomes evidence of repeatable leadership.

Every case study on your site has items 1, 3, and (partially) 6 implicit. Almost none have items 2, 4, 5, and 7 made explicit. Fixing that across three case studies in six weeks would materially change the read.

### Surface a "scoreboard" at the top of every case study

You already have a `project-meta` strip (Role / Brands / Team / Impact). Keep the layout, upgrade the contents. Replace "Impact: Bi-annual Seasonal Collections" with "Impact: 7 brands · 35% faster speed-to-market · 6+ seasonal launches." Hiring managers scan this strip first and often leave the page if it doesn't earn their time.

## 3. Visual Sophistication — Typography, Color, Whitespace

The current design is pleasant, consistent, and competent. For a creative-role hiring manager it is still the layer they judge hardest — your taste is being read through the site itself, not just the work on it. Three moves would lift the perceived altitude without requiring a rebuild.

### Color palette

The palette I can read from your inline styles is a terracotta accent (`#cc6d47`) over warm off-whites (`#f9f7f4`, `#fefdfb`), a near-black (`#1a1a1a`), and warm greys. This is a 2020-era "artisanal craft" palette — think Kinfolk, boutique hotel, small-batch roaster. It is beautiful, but it connotes curator, not director.

A Director-tier palette tends to do one of two things:

- **Severity:** a near-monochrome system with one sharp accent. Think Bottega Veneta (near-black on paper-white with a single chrome-green moment), or Aimé Leon Dore's disciplined taupe palette. This says *authority through confidence*.
- **Ownable neutrality:** an unusual neutral that reads as "this is my palette." Think Mejuri's bone/putty, or Hermès's orange-as-neutral. This says *ownership through specificity*.

Both are available to you. Terracotta can stay if you commit harder — make it the proprietary neutral, not the accent. Drop the warm off-white gradient backgrounds (they soften everything) and let terracotta carry structural weight with a disciplined companion neutral (bone, charcoal, or a single chromatic black). Right now terracotta is doing the job of an eyebrow color; it deserves more air.

### Typography

Playfair Display is the most overused serif of the last decade. It shows up on every wedding site, every Squarespace template, and every "premium" lookbook. For a creative leader's site, it is an anti-signal — it reads as "I picked a Google Font that signals 'tasteful' rather than a typeface that signals me."

Swap it. Two directions, depending on where you want to land:

- **Editorial authority:** a modern display serif with a clear point of view. Good picks: GT Super, Tiempos Headline, Domaine Display, Canela.
- **Confident neutral:** a single strong sans with editorial intent. Good picks: GT America, Söhne, Neue Haas Grotesk, ABC Diatype. This is often the stronger move for a site that wants to feel like a brand system rather than a layout.

Either way, the body font needs to stop being the system UI stack. The Apple/Segoe system stack is a developer default, not a designer's typographic decision. Pair your display face with a specific text face (e.g., Söhne + Söhne Mono, or Tiempos Text + GT America). On a creative's site, the body typeface *is* part of the portfolio.

Also: shrink your H1s. The hero H1 and the About H1 are both sized for emotional impact. Tight H1s read as certain; loud ones read as junior.

### Whitespace and layout

This part is mostly working. The section padding is generous, the grids are clean, and the case studies use full-bleed imagery well. Three refinements that would lift the whole system:

- **Cards feel SaaS.** The `border-radius: 16px` + soft shadow + 1px warm-grey border pattern is everywhere: experience cards, skill cards, contact cards, highlight boxes. It reads as a template. A Director-grade site either removes the card entirely (let type and whitespace do the work) or commits to a distinctive card treatment (e.g., sharp corners with a 1pt rule, or full pill radius, but not soft shadow + border + radius all at once).
- **The hero stats module is a resume trope.** Three big numbers with labels is what every portfolio does. If you keep the module, differentiate it — put the numbers in a thin serif ruled line, for example, rather than big bold digits. If you replace the stats entirely (recommended, per Section 1), this module can go.
- **Quote blocks.** Italic 2rem serif with uppercase byline is a 2018 pattern. The cumulative effect across six pages is decorative rather than substantive. Either cut them or redesign around a distinctive typographic treatment.

### Micro signals that hiring managers register subconsciously

- The favicon is a generic SVG. A Director-grade site has a wordmark favicon — even a confident "TW" monogram — that also appears in the nav and on social share cards.
- The Open Graph image (`og-image.jpg`) is doing the heavy lifting when you're shared on LinkedIn or in a recruiter DM. Make sure the OG card is a designed asset with a positioning line, not a hero photo fallback.
- The `© 2026` footer is bare. A Director-grade footer usually includes: a short positioning line, LinkedIn, an email, and optionally a "last updated" signal so recruiters know the site is actively maintained.

## 4. User Experience — the 2-Minute Hiring Manager Scan

Pretend I'm a Head of Brand or a creative recruiter with 120 seconds. Here is what I get from the site right now, and what I need but don't get.

### What I get today

1. Senior Design Director, 15+ years, heritage brands (hero).
2. Brand logo wall confirms scale.
3. Six case studies with nice thumbnails and strong imagery.
4. An About page with experience, skills, and personal notes.
5. A Contact page with email, LinkedIn, and an availability line.

That is a competent portfolio.

### What's missing

**A "start here" signal.** Six case studies is a lot at the shortlist stage. A scanner needs to know which one to read first. Pick one hero case study (my vote: Damen + Hastings, once it's numbers-rich — it is your most differentiating story) and surface it above the six-up grid as "Signature Work" or "Featured Case." The other five become supporting evidence.

**A way to leave with a document.** Every Director-level search process produces a PDF that gets forwarded internally to a hiring manager, a VP, or a panel. Your site should offer a "Download CV" or "Download portfolio" PDF — a 4–6 page designed document with the positioning line, three case studies, bio, and contact. Right now a recruiter has to build that themselves, and they'll build it from LinkedIn instead of your site.

**One piece of soft proof.** At VP level you need press and speaking engagements. At Director level, a single testimonial or LinkedIn recommendation pulled onto the site does the same work. Even one quote — "Trevor is the rare creative leader who treats merchandising as a partner rather than an obstacle." — from a peer or a former manager changes how the site reads.

**Business-shape context on the homepage.** A scanner in 120 seconds needs to register the *shape* of your environment: how many brands, what geographies, how big the team, how large the portfolio. Today this is buried in About and underweighted.

**A clear CTA in the nav.** "Contact" is a nav link. Make it a button ("Let's talk" or "Start a conversation") or style it distinctly from the others. High-intent visitors (recruiters) should have an unmistakable path to email.

### Navigation frictions

- The dropdown menu of case studies is hover-based, unreliable on touchpads and broken on touch screens. Since the homepage already grids the six case studies, consider collapsing the dropdown to a single "Work" link that scrolls to or opens the grid.
- "Trevor Wills" in the nav is plain text. A monogram wordmark (custom, not Playfair) would add the one designed asset that lives on every page.
- "Beyond Work" on the About page (Detroit sports, Fujifilm, weekend camping) is warm and human. At Director tier this is actually a modest *asset* — it helps the culture-fit conversation — but it currently sits at the same visual weight as your experience and skills. Shrink it to one paragraph and keep it where it is, or move it to its own `/personal` route.
- There are two subtly different navigation patterns on the site (the dropdown + the inline link). Pick one.

### Accessibility and trust signals

- The hero eyebrow color `#cc6d47` over near-white background sits around ~3.4:1 contrast for small text — borderline on WCAG AA. Darken the terracotta by a notch for body uses, or reserve it for display sizes where 3:1 contrast is allowed.
- Several carousel images have generic alt text ("Sample review sessions"). Either make them descriptive or mark them decorative.
- A single line in the footer about analytics ("This site uses analytics to understand visitor traffic") costs nothing and signals professional polish.

## 5. Technical Build & Content Gaps

The technical build is healthier than most portfolios at this level. You have Open Graph tags, canonical URLs, JSON-LD schema, a sitemap, `robots.txt`, image dimension attributes, lazy loading, and font preconnect hints. That is real work. Protect it.

### Technical hygiene

- **Title-tag inconsistency.** Schema.org says "Senior Design Director," OG tags say "Creative Strategy Director," hero eyebrow says "Senior Design Director," About says "Creative Strategy Director." Pick one, apply site-wide.
- **`case-study-walmart-assessment.html` has a duplicate `<title>` tag.** Remove it.
- **The `SS27Trend/` subfolder** sits in your repo root next to your live pages but isn't linked from the main nav. If it's the supporting artifact for the AI case study, keep it; if it's stale scaffolding, cut it from the deploy.
- **`PORTFOLIO-AUDIT-REPORT.md` is in the project root.** Make sure it's excluded from the production deploy. Same for this file if you put it in the same folder.
- **Analytics is loaded four times** (once per page head). Fine functionally, but moving GA to a single include or tag manager makes future swaps trivial.
- **No CV/portfolio PDF download path.** The single most-requested artifact from recruiters. Add a `/portfolio.pdf` or `/cv.pdf` and link it from the nav, the hero, and the Contact page.

### Content gaps that actually matter at Director tier

A Leader's Dossier at the Director tier is lighter than at VP — you don't need speaking engagements and published essays to be credible. But a short list of additions meaningfully changes the shortlist read. Ranked by leverage.

**1. Two or three testimonials or pull quotes.** This is the single highest-leverage addition for a Director candidate. One from a peer or manager (for leadership), one from a direct report (for team-building), and one from a cross-functional partner like a merchandiser or licensor (for soft power). You do not need formal reference quotes — LinkedIn recommendations, cited with attribution, work fine.

**2. A short "What I'm good at / How I work" block on the homepage.** Not a published essay — a typographically strong, three-to-five-line statement of stance. This is what turns a portfolio into a brand at Director level. See the example at the end of Section 1.

**3. An explicit team-leadership snapshot.** Right now team leadership is asserted ("10+ designers mentored") but not detailed. Replace with: number of direct reports, where they sit (US offices, Shanghai, Hong Kong), promotions under your tenure, and — if favorable — retention. The sentence "Direct-reports: 4 senior designers in the US and China; promoted 4 designers to senior roles and 2 to director in the last five years" is worth more than any soft paragraph.

**4. A downloadable CV or portfolio PDF.** Already mentioned above. A 4–6 page designed document saves a recruiter 20 minutes and dramatically raises your hit rate in internal submissions.

**5. Named cross-functional partnerships.** The About page mentions collaboration with merchandising, product development, and operations. Make one or two of them specific: "Partnered with the VP of Merchandising at [Brand] to realign the accessories assortment for [year] — contributed to [outcome]." Named partners and named outcomes are evidence of soft power.

**6. Your AI Trailblazers role, properly framed.** You lead AI adoption at your company. At Director tier, this is a differentiator — most candidates on any Creative Director shortlist are not yet fluent in AI implementation. A short block on the About page or a rewritten AI case study that makes this organizational (not personal-project) changes your read considerably.

**7. A "Selective availability" reframe of the Contact page.** "Currently exploring senior leadership roles — let's connect" reads as open-market. Compare:

> Selective availability in 2026. I'm in conversation with a small number of teams about Creative Director, Group Creative Director, and Brand Director roles. If you're building or restructuring a creative org, I'd like to hear about it.

That second version makes you the scarce asset. It's a confident move that works at Director tier without overreaching.

### What you do *not* need to add

A few things the prior (VP-focused) version of this audit pushed for are optional at Director tier and probably not worth your time right now:

- A published leadership-philosophy essay. Nice if it happens naturally; not required.
- A speaking-engagements shelf. If one exists, list it. Don't manufacture it.
- Executive education credentials (Kellogg, Wharton, IE). Defensibly optional at Director level.
- A press/media mentions section. Not required at this tier.

Save that effort for the rewrite of two or three case studies — it will move the needle much more.

## Cut, Amplify, Refine — the decision matrix

### Cut

- The current hero H1 ("I'm Trevor Wills"). Smallest effort, largest lift on the site.
- The `15+ / 7+ / 10+` stats module as currently framed.
- The Eames quote on the homepage closer. Replace with your short "How I work" block.
- The AI-Powered Trend Presentation case study as currently written, or demote it from the main grid to a "Notes" section.
- The "Beyond Work" section at current weight. Shrink to one paragraph or move to `/personal`.
- The hover dropdown in the nav. Collapse to a single "Work" link.
- Playfair Display on the quote blocks. Longer-term, swap the whole display face.
- The duplicate `<title>` tag on the Walmart case study.
- Any audit files or dev artifacts from the production deploy.

### Amplify

- The Digital Product Creation case study's template (stats showcase + decision narrative). Rebuild every other case study against it.
- The Damen + Hastings story. This is your most differentiating evidence at Director tier — it shows you've actually *built* a brand, not just directed one. Give it a numeric scoreboard. Consider elevating it to "Signature Work" position on the homepage.
- The brand logo wall. It's doing more work for your positioning than you're using it for. A single-line positioning claim above it ("Creative direction for:") and more real estate for the logos would help.
- Your AI Trailblazers angle. Frame it as organizational leadership, not a personal project. This is a genuine differentiator at Director tier.
- Cross-functional specifics. Name the VPs and partners you've worked with. Named collaboration makes soft power visible.
- Team leadership specifics. Replace "10+ designers mentored" with named promotions, tenure, geography, and retention.

### Refine

- The positioning title. Pick one (*Senior Design Director* or *Creative Strategy Director*) and unify it across meta tags, JSON-LD, hero eyebrow, About, and social.
- The color system. Commit harder to terracotta or replace it with a more disciplined neutral. Remove the warm gradient background from quote blocks.
- The type system. Invest in one distinctive display face + one distinctive body face. Stop using the system UI stack as body.
- The case study template. Move to the seven-part skeleton: Context, Mandate, Decision, Collaboration, Team, Outcome, What-it-changed.
- The scoreboard strip at the top of each case study. Real numbers replace generic labels.
- The footer. Add a positioning line, LinkedIn, email, and a "last updated" indicator.
- The Contact availability line. Reframe from "exploring" to "selective availability."

## The order I would do this in

If you tackled these in about four weeks of part-time work, the highest-leverage sequence is:

1. **Week 1 — Positioning.** Rewrite the hero. Unify titles site-wide. Add the "How I work" block to the homepage. Replace the stats module. Reframe the Contact availability line. No design work yet.
2. **Week 2 — Signature case study.** Pick one (Damen + Hastings is the strongest bet) and rebuild it against the seven-part skeleton with real numbers. This becomes your template.
3. **Week 3 — Supporting case studies + testimonials.** Rewrite Heritage Brands and Digital Product Creation against the same skeleton. Pull two or three testimonials from LinkedIn recommendations and add them to the site.
4. **Week 4 — Visual system + CV.** Swap Playfair for a chosen display face. Tighten H1s. Rebuild the card style. Ship a designed `/portfolio.pdf`. Fix the terracotta contrast. Launch.

You won't get everything perfect in four weeks. You don't need to. The first two weeks are where 60% of the upward read comes from, and almost all of that is writing and positioning, not design.

You are already a credible Director-tier candidate on paper — the roster, the tenure, the entrepreneurial credential, and the AI adoption work are all real. What the site currently does is *recap* that credibility. What a Director-grade site does is *assert* it.
